# Fieldwork Ethos

Product management exists because building the wrong thing is expensive. That hasn't changed. What has changed is how fast you can build.

AI agents collapse execution time. A prototype that took a sprint now takes an afternoon. That's an asset, but it also means you can be wrong faster and at greater scale. The discipline matters more, not less.

Fieldwork is a PM workflow built for that reality. It keeps the parts of the process that still earn their cost, cuts the parts that don't, and treats fast experimentation as a first-class tool rather than a shortcut.

---

## Principles

**Match the process to the bet.** A small experiment doesn't need a spec. A chunky feature with cross-functional dependencies does. The workflow scales to the decision. Know which one you're in.

**Start with the problem or opportunity.** A spec that doesn't trace back to a real customer need or a genuine market opportunity is a liability. Let that anchor constrain the solution. Don't skip past it.

**Run it, don't map it.** When you can spin up a prototype in an afternoon, the right response to an unknown is often to test it, not document it. Fieldwork is not anti-rigour. It's anti-unnecessary-rigour.

**Grounded or useless.** Every output references your actual product, your actual users, your actual codebase. If it could have been written for any team, it wasn't written for yours.

**Gates are bet-sizing moments.** The hard gates in Fieldwork exist to ask one question: is this bet big enough to warrant the next step? They are not bureaucracy. Skipping them doesn't make you faster. It makes you faster at building the wrong thing.

**Shipping is not done when code merges.** GTM, launch, and retro are part of the workflow. A feature without a plan for how users find it, and without capturing what you learned, is half a feature.
